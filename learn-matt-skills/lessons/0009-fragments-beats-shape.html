Lesson 0009 · Writing workflow · Usage

Start with fragments. Then pick how you want to steer.

Fragments, beats, and shape aren't three steps you do in order. Fragments help you gather raw material. Beats and shape are two different ways to turn that material into a finished article — you pick one, not both.

The rule worth remembering Use fragments while you're still figuring out what you actually think. Once you feel like you have enough material, stop collecting and choose either beats or shape to write the article. Don't run both — each one is a complete path from raw material to finished draft.

1. writing-fragments: collect, don't organize

Think of this as a conversation that pulls interesting things out of your head: sharp sentences, claims, scenes, examples, complaints, half-formed thoughts, quotes, or clusters of related ideas. The best thing it can surface is a leading word — a single compact name or metaphor that could anchor the whole piece.

The agent keeps asking questions and appends every useful fragment to a single Markdown file, separated by horizontal rules. There's no outline, no section headings, no attempt to make the file readable to anyone else yet. That deliberate messiness is the point — structure comes later.

$writing-fragments

I want to explore why agent skills feel more reliable than giant prompts.
Save fragments to writing/skills-reliability-fragments.md.

While you're in a session, you can say things like cut the last one, rewrite that sharper, or merge those two. Stop when the questions start circling back to things you've already said — not when you have an outline.

2. Pick your writing mode

Pick thisWhen your material wantsHow it works
writing-beatsA narrative with pivots, reveals, scenes, or multiple possible directionsThe agent offers you 2–3 possible next beats; you pick one; it writes just that beat, then offers the next set of choices
writing-shapeA coherent argument where paragraph order and formatting need to be carefully worked outThe agent offers 2–3 possible openings; then asks what the reader needs to hear next, growing the draft one block at a time

A simple rule of thumb: use shape when you're writing a technical explainer or building an argument. Use beats when the sequence of ideas and how the reader experiences them is the main challenge. Still not sure? Ask yourself: "Do I want to choose the next move in a story, or defend the next paragraph in an argument?"

Both modes share one constraint: grounding

Before either skill starts writing, you establish what your audience already knows — the prerequisites. After that, every new concept has to be introduced before any later section relies on it. That's grounding.

For example, you can't use "context load" to explain something unless the reader either already knew that term coming in, or an earlier paragraph defined it. If the next thing you want to write depends on a concept the reader hasn't seen yet, you need to either write a paragraph that introduces it first, or add it to the prerequisites.

Why this matters Your raw fragments are a quarry, not a script. Both skills are free to split, merge, rephrase, or skip fragments entirely. The article is done when its journey or argument is complete — not when every fragment has been used up.

Argument shaping

$writing-shape

Use writing/skills-reliability-fragments.md as read-only raw material.

Audience: software engineers who use coding agents but do not author skills.

Including the raw-material path and save path up front means the skills won't need to ask you for setup details. During either session, feel free to edit the article file directly whenever you want — the agent re-reads it before each append and will preserve your changes.

Quick check: pick the right tool

You have a topic, but you don't know your strongest claim yet.

You're still figuring out what you think — that means you're in explore mode. Use writing-fragments and resist the urge to outline.

You have rich material and want to choose among dramatic pivots.

Multiple possible directions through the material means writing-beats — you pick one reachable beat at a time.

You have rich material and want a rigorous technical explanation.

Building an argument paragraph by paragraph with deliberate formatting points to writing-shape.

Try it yourself

Start with something small — don't use your most precious idea for a first run. Invoke $writing-fragments with a single sentence and a save path. Stay in exploration mode for at least ten questions. Then stop, and pick exactly one of the two writing skills using the rule above.
